# Deep learning without backpropagation: the theory

Deep learning in PyHGF looks unusual at first sight: no loss function is ever
differentiated, no automatic-differentiation graph is built, and yet the
networks train — in a specific configuration they reproduce backpropagation's
weight updates to numerical precision. This page explains why, from the ground
up, assuming no prior familiarity with PyHGF, predictive coding, or the
mathematics of learning.

It covers how deep networks normally learn (*backpropagation*), how a
*predictive-coding* network learns instead, the theorem saying when the two
coincide, the regime where predictive coding does something genuinely different
(*prospective configuration*), and where PyHGF sits on that map.

```{mermaid}
flowchart LR
    BP["Backpropagation<br/><i>global chain rule</i>"]
    PC["Classical predictive coding<br/><i>local errors, settling beliefs</i>"]
    SIL["Silent interior<br/>PC = backprop"]
    PRO["Moving interior<br/>prospective configuration"]
    PYHGF["PyHGF<br/><i>single-sweep PC,<br/>confidence-weighted</i>"]
    BP -->|"same gradients, different derivation"| PC
    PC --> SIL
    PC --> PRO
    SIL --> PYHGF
    PRO -.->|"open direction"| PYHGF
```

Two companion pages complete the picture: the
[DeepNetwork implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)
shows how these ideas become code — up to a full Transformer trained without
backpropagation — and the [deep-learning tutorial](0.7-Deep_learning.ipynb)
walks through a worked classification example. The belief-updating machinery
that everything below builds on (prediction errors, precisions, the

## From backpropagation to PyHGF

## 1. Backpropagation: how deep networks normally learn

A **deep network** is a stack of layers. Each layer holds a vector of
**activations** (numbers describing what the layer currently represents) and
is connected to the next by a matrix of **weights** — the adjustable
parameters that the network learns. Data enter at one end, are transformed
layer by layer (each layer multiplies by its weights and applies a
nonlinearity), and a prediction comes out the other end. A **loss function**
measures how wrong that prediction is.

Training means adjusting every weight to make the loss smaller. The central
tool is the **gradient**: for each weight, the derivative of the loss with
respect to that weight — a number saying how much (and in which direction)
the loss would change if the weight moved a little. **Gradient descent**
repeatedly nudges every weight a small step against its gradient.

The gradients themselves come from **backpropagation**: an application of the
calculus **chain rule** (the rule for differentiating nested functions) that
sweeps backward through the network. Backpropagation computes each weight's
gradient from the error at the layer below it and the activity at the layer
above, obtaining those errors by the chain rule, sweeping backward, with the
activations held fixed at their forward-pass values. It is a *global*
procedure: the backward sweep threads information from the loss through every
layer, so no weight's update can be computed from purely local quantities.

## 2. Classical predictive coding

**Predictive coding** (PC) is a different way to organise the same
computation, drawn from theoretical neuroscience. Instead of activations, each
layer holds **beliefs**: a belief is a guess (a **mean**, written $\mu$) plus
a confidence in that guess (a **precision**, written $\pi$ — the inverse of a
variance, so high precision means high confidence). Each layer *predicts* the
layer below it; the prediction it sends down is written $\hat{\mu}$ (the hat
always marks a predicted quantity). The mismatch between what a layer
predicted and what it observed is the **prediction error**

$$
\delta = \mu - \hat{\mu}.
$$

How beliefs are corrected from prediction errors, weighted by precision, is
the subject of the [theory notebook](0.1-Theory.ipynb); here only the shape of
the network matters. Write $\mu_\ell$ for the value belief of layer $\ell$
(layer $0$ is the output, larger $\ell$ is deeper toward the input),
$W_\ell$ for the weights connecting layer $\ell+1$ to layer $\ell$, and $g$
for the **coupling function** — the nonlinearity a layer applies when it
predicts the layer below (a bias is folded into $W_\ell$ as an always-on
constant input). Predictions flow top to bottom:

$$
\hat{\mu}_\ell = W_\ell \, g(\mu_{\ell+1}).
$$

The whole network is scored by one quantity, the **variational free energy**
$F$ — for Gaussian beliefs, simply the sum of every layer's squared prediction
error weighted by its precision:

$$
F = \sum_\ell \frac{\pi_\ell}{2}\,\big(\mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell\big)^2 .
$$

Learning happens in two nested motions, both descending this same $F$:

1. **Beliefs settle.** *Clamp* the observation on the output layer (fix
   $\mu_0 = y$; a clamped observation is just a belief that is not allowed to
   move), hold the weights fixed, and let every interior belief slide down the
   free energy, $\dot{\mu}_\ell = -\partial F / \partial \mu_\ell$. Each
   belief is pulled toward its own top-down prediction and pushed by the
   prediction error of the layer below, gated by the local slope of $g$. In
   the textbook variant this relaxation runs for many iterations, until the
   beliefs reach an equilibrium.
2. **Weights nudge.** Each connection then adjusts from purely *local*
   quantities — the precision-weighted error on one side,
   $e_\ell := \pi_\ell\,(\mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell) = \pi_\ell\,\delta_\ell$,
   and the activity on the other:

$$
\Delta W_\ell \;\propto\; e_\ell \; g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top .
$$

This weight rule is **Hebbian** — "cells that fire together wire together":
the update to a connection is a product of the two quantities available at its
two ends. Nothing in it sweeps backward through the network; no part ever
sees the whole model. That locality is the point of predictive coding — and
it raises the obvious question: can such local nudges possibly match the
gradients that backpropagation's global sweep computes?

(pc-equals-backprop)=
## 3. When predictive coding equals backpropagation

The answer is yes — under one condition. This is not a quirk of any
implementation; it is a specific, well-understood point in the landscape of
predictive-coding models. This section explains in plain terms *why* the two
coincide, *when* they must, and *when* predictive coding does something
different.

(silent-interior)=
### 3.1 The one condition: a silent interior

Predictive coding reproduces exactly backpropagation's gradients under a
single condition: **the hidden activities stay at their forward-pass values
even after the observation is clamped on the output.** Call this a *silent
interior*. When the interior is silent, the only real prediction error in the
network is at the output; every interior layer merely relays the forward
pass; and each connection's local update — (error on one side) × (activity on
the other) — is precisely the chain-rule gradient. If instead the hidden
layers moved to satisfy the target, their errors would no longer be
backpropagation's errors, and the two would diverge. This is the classical
condition of {cite:t}`whittington:2017`, and the parity recipe of the
[implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md) (its *Verifying a
port* section) is one way to force it.

:::{note}
"Silent" does not mean *frozen*. The interior still shifts by an
infinitesimal amount — just enough to carry the gradient onward. In the
precision picture that shift is the routed error divided by a huge precision,
and the precision-weighted weight update multiplies it back to full size.
Silent means the movement is too small to change *what the activations
represent*, not that it is literally zero.
:::

```{prf:theorem} Silent-interior predictive coding reproduces backpropagation
:label: thm-pc-backprop

Clamp the observation $\mu_0 = y$ at the output, hold the weights fixed, and
let the beliefs slide down the free energy of §2
($\dot{\mu}_\ell = -\partial F / \partial \mu_\ell$). Writing the
precision-weighted error $e_\ell := \pi_\ell\,(\mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell)$,
the motion stops ($\dot{\mu}_\ell = 0$) exactly where

$$
e_\ell \;=\; g'(\hat{\mu}_\ell) \odot \big(W_{\ell-1}^\top\, e_{\ell-1}\big)
\tag{★}
$$

with the slope $g'$ taken at the prediction. Equation (★) *is*
backpropagation's error recursion: the output error $e_0$ seeds it (for a
squared-error output, the negative loss gradient; for a categorical head,
$\text{one-hot} - \text{softmax}$ — the categorical-head rule of the porting
recipe in the [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)),
and (★) carries it up the stack layer by layer. The single thing (★) needs in
order to equal backpropagation is that every $\hat{\mu}_\ell$ it is built
from stays at its feedforward value — the **silent interior**. Under it,
$e_\ell$ equals the backpropagation error $\delta_\ell$ at every layer, so
the local Hebbian weight step — descent on the same $F$ —

$$
\Delta W_\ell \;\propto\; e_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top
\;=\; \delta_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top
$$

reproduces the backpropagation gradient node for node.
```

The correspondence is a provable statement about the network's relaxed fixed
point. {cite:t}`whittington:2017` reach the silent interior in the limit of
small output error (the feedback barely perturbs the interior);
{cite:t}`song:2020` make it *exact* by holding the predictions at their
feedforward values during the relaxation; and {cite:t}`millidge:2020` extend
the result to arbitrary computation graphs. In PyHGF the slope convention of
(★) matches
the update kernels, which evaluate $g'$ and $g''$ at `expected_mean`
(`vectorized_posterior_update_*`); the Gaussian free energy and the precision
(confidence) terms are derived in the [theory notebook](0.1-Theory.ipynb).

:::{admonition} Why this is not a reimplementation of backpropagation
:class: important

The parity recipe forces the silent interior with the confidence lever
(§3.2): pinning the interior precision to $\pi_0 = 10^4$ lets the mean update
$\mu_\ell = \hat{\mu}_\ell + e_\ell / \pi_\ell$ move a belief by only the
messenger shift $e_\ell / \pi_0$ (see the note above), while the
`precision_weighted` gradient $\delta \otimes a \cdot \hat{\pi}_\text{child}$
(`pyhgf.updates.vectorized.learning`) multiplies the same $\pi_\ell$ back in —
the two cancel, and $\Delta W_\ell$ lands on
$\delta_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top$. Dropped to leading order in $1/\pi_0$,
the whole update is one reverse sweep —
$\delta_\ell = g'(\hat{\mu}_\ell) \odot (W_{\ell-1}^\top \delta_{\ell-1})$
then $\Delta W_\ell \propto \delta_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top$ — the same
reverse-mode vector–Jacobian product `jax.grad` would build. But PyHGF never
differentiates a graph; it reaches those numbers by local message passing on
a generative model. The equivalence is therefore a theorem-level coincidence
of two derivations — variational inference on one side, the chain rule on the
other — not one reimplementing the other, and it holds *only* in the
silent-interior limit. Release the precisions, the interior moves, (★)
breaks, and the update leaves backpropagation behind (§4).
:::

The result is concrete: in the pinned configuration, one PyHGF step
reproduces backpropagation to ~1e-5 relative error — the parity numbers of
the *Verifying a port* section, and the reason the worked Transformer of the
[implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md) follows its
backpropagation twin's loss curve so closely.

:::{admonition} What is established, and what is specific to PyHGF
:class: seealso

The equivalence itself is established prior work: that silent-interior
predictive coding reproduces backpropagation is the theorem of
{cite:t}`whittington:2017`, made exact by {cite:t}`song:2020` and extended to
arbitrary computation graphs by {cite:t}`millidge:2020`. PyHGF neither
discovers nor reimplements that result. What is specific here is the *route* to
it — the equivalence is expressed through the Hierarchical Gaussian Filter's
own confidence (precision) machinery, where a single precision both silences
the interior and rescales the frozen shift back to a full-size gradient (§3.2),
and it is reached in a *single sweep* (predict once, correct once, nudge once)
rather than the textbook settle-to-equilibrium loop. The contribution is the
confidence framing and the map of where the silent regime ends (§4–§5), not the
backpropagation correspondence.
:::

(two-levers)=
### 3.2 Two ways to silence the interior

Two levers produce a silent interior, and they are the same physics seen from
opposite sides.

- **Turn up confidence (the toolbox's lever).** A belief's updated value is a
  confidence-weighted compromise between its top-down prediction (its
  forward-pass value) and the bottom-up error (see the precision terms in the
  [theory notebook](0.1-Theory.ipynb)). Set the hidden precision enormous and
  the compromise sticks to the prediction: the activities cannot move. This
  is an *explicit* dial — the $10^4$ in the parity recipe of the
  [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md).
- **Make the network wide and shallow (the scaling lever).** With ordinary
  unit confidences, whether the settled activities land near the forward pass
  depends on the network's shape. A scaling analysis of predictive coding
  {cite:p}`innocenti:2026` shows that the settled
  ("equilibrated") energy PC learns on is a *rescaled* loss
  $L(\theta) / s(\theta)$, with a rescaling
  $s(\theta) = 1 + \Theta(\text{depth} / \text{width})$. When width far
  exceeds depth, $s \to 1$, the rescaled loss becomes the plain loss, and
  PC's gradients converge to backpropagation's. Wide-and-shallow silences the
  interior by geometry rather than by decree.

The confidence dial and the rescaling $s(\theta)$ are the same knob — "how
strongly the interior can be pulled off the forward pass." The toolbox makes
it explicit; the scaling result lets it emerge from architecture. The worked
Transformer of the [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)
is wide (64–128 features) and shallow (two blocks), so it sits in the
$s \to 1$, silent-interior basin on both counts — which is why it matches its
backpropagation twin so cleanly.

(prospective-configuration)=
## 4. The opposite end: prospective configuration

Is a silent interior a limitation? It is the *opposite* of a distinct idea,
**prospective configuration** {cite:p}`Song2024`: letting the hidden
activities relax to a genuinely new, target-consistent configuration *before*
any weight changes. That reconfiguration is argued to reduce interference
between updates and to help online and continual learning — behaviour
backpropagation, which never moves its activations, cannot express.

The two are not in conflict; they are the two ends of one dial:

- **Silent interior** (activities barely move) → PC = backpropagation → *no*
  prospective configuration.
- **Moving interior** (activities reconfigure) → PC ≠ backpropagation →
  prospective configuration, with its potential benefits.

The difference is the *scale* of movement. The silent regime allows only
*messenger* movement — the infinitesimal shift that transmits the gradient
(§3.1). Prospective configuration needs *reconfiguration* movement —
substantial enough to rearrange the representation toward the target. A
network cannot do both at once: reproducing backpropagation requires
silencing the interior; prospective configuration requires the reverse.

## 5. When predictive coding actually beats backpropagation

There is one regime where PC provably learns in *fewer* steps than
backpropagation: **faster saddle-to-saddle escape in deeper-than-wide
networks.** Deep networks trained from a small initialisation descend the
loss in a staircase — long flat plateaus (**saddle points**, where the
gradient nearly vanishes and training crawls) punctuated by sudden drops.
Because PC learns on the rescaled loss $L / s(\theta)$, its gradient carries
an extra term that is nonzero *even where the plain loss is flat*, so it
slides off those plateaus sooner: the degenerate, slow-to-escape saddles of
the loss become benign in the rescaled energy {cite:p}`innocenti:2024`.

This is the mirror image of §3.2 — the advantage needs $s(\theta)$ far from
1, i.e. **depth greater than width**, the very regime the silent-interior
condition excludes. It comes with hard limits: it relies on the standard
parameterisation, which is numerically unstable at scale, and it
**disappears for residual networks**, so it does not apply to transformers
however deep — the residual shortcuts remove the saddles it exploits.

## 6. Where PyHGF sits

PyHGF is *single-sweep* predictive coding — predict once, correct beliefs
once, nudge weights once per batch, rather than the textbook
settle-to-equilibrium loop — with the confidence machinery kept explicit
throughout. On the map above:

- **Parity is an anchor, not a ceiling to beat.** The parity recipe
  deliberately parks the learners at the silent-interior end, where PC =
  backpropagation. That is what makes it a rigorous correctness check (the
  *Verifying a port* section of the
  [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)) — and also why
  nothing here should be expected to *out-learn* backpropagation. Matching it
  is the goal, and matching it is what the numbers show (the worked
  Transformer).
- **The worked Transformer is in the flat basin by construction.** Being
  wide, shallow, and residual puts it squarely where PC = backpropagation.
  Widening it keeps it there; deepening it does *not* open the saddle-escape
  advantage, because residual models lack that regime (§5). Distinctive PC
  behaviour will not appear by tuning this model.
- **LayerNorm is frozen for a concrete reason.** Making LayerNorm a genuine
  settling belief layer breaks the correspondence even at large width — the
  normalisation's coupling across a token disturbs the settling the
  silent-interior condition depends on {cite:p}`innocenti:2026`. Keeping it a
  frozen relay (see the worked Transformer) sidesteps this; the paths to let
  its affine learn without reintroducing the problem are open questions.
- **Exploring PC's own behaviour means leaving the silent regime on
  purpose.** Prospective configuration, faster adaptation, and interference
  resistance all live at the *moving-interior* end. Reaching it needs three
  things the current scheme lacks: released precisions (so the interior can
  move), a settling loop (so it relaxes to an equilibrium instead of taking
  one sweep), and non-stationary or continual tasks (where the benefit would
  show). These are open directions — and the reason the confidence machinery,
  a parity device today, is kept.
