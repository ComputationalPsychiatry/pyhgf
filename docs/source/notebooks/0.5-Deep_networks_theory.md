# Learning in deep predictive coding networks - The theory

The error back-propagation algorithm can be approximated in networks of
neurons, in which plasticity only depends on the activity of presynaptic
and postsynaptic neurons {cite:p}`Whittington2019`.

Deep networks optimisation on ordinary supervised tasks such as
classification and regression has been dominated by backpropagation. 
Predictive coding (PC) has offered an alternative framework inspired by biological learning
and proved efficiency in tasks typically faced by biological organisms.
Such networks can also be built and trained in PyHGF, from multilayer perceptrons
to convolutional and recurrent, up to full Transformer architectures.
What makes them unusual is *how* they learn: using a combination of belief updating, precision-weighted prediction error 
propagation and Hebbian weight learning. In a specific configuration, the resulting weight
updates reproduce backpropagation's to numerical precision.

This page explores why, assuming no prior familiarity with PyHGF, predictive
coding, or the mathematics of learning. We cover 1. how deep networks normally learn
(§1), 2. how a predictive-coding network learns instead (§2), 3. the theorem
saying when the two coincide and its proof (§3), 4. the regimes where
predictive coding does something genuinely different (§4–§5), and 5. where
PyHGF sits on that map (§6).

Several companion pages complete the picture of this deep learning chapter: the
[DeepNetwork implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)
shows how these ideas become code, the [deep-learning tutorial](0.7-Deep_learning.ipynb)
walks through a worked classification example and the the [transformer section](0.8-Transformers.ipynb) 
show how to scale this to complex architectures like Transformers.

## 1. Backpropagation: learning in deep neural networks

A **deep network** is a stack of neural layers. Each layer holds a vector of
**activations** (numbers describing what the layer currently represents) and
is connected to the next by a matrix of **weights**, the adjustable
parameters that the network learns. Data $x$ enter at one end, are transformed
layer by layer (each layer multiplies by its weights and applies a
nonlinearity), and a prediction $\hat{y}$ comes out the other end. A **loss function**
measures how wrong that prediction is compared to an observed outcome $y$.

Training the neural network means adjusting every weight to make the loss smaller. 
The central tool is the **gradient**: for each weight, the derivative of the loss with
respect to that weight (i.e. how much and in which direction the loss would
change if the weight moved a little). **Gradient descent** repeatedly nudges
every weight a small step against its gradient.

```{note}
In the rest of this document, we index layers by $\ell$, with **layer $0$ the output** and 
$\ell$ growing toward the input. We write $\mu_\ell$ and $\hat{\mu}_\ell$ for the forward and backward
activations of layer $\ell$, $W_\ell$ for the matrix of weights connecting layer $\ell+1$ to layer $\ell$, 
and $g$ for the nonlinearity (a bias is folded into $W_\ell$ as an always-on constant input).
```

The forward pass computes, layer by layer,

$$
\hat{\mu}_\ell = W_\ell \, g(\mu_{\ell+1}),
$$ (eq-forward)

the hat on $\hat{\mu}_\ell$ marking a *predicted / computed* quantity. For an input $x$, 
a target $y$, and the standard squared error, the loss is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}\lVert f(x) - y \rVert^2$
with $f(x)$ the network's output.

The gradients themselves come from **backpropagation**: an application of the
calculus **chain rule** (the rule for differentiating nested functions) that
sweeps backward through the network. An early weight affects the loss only
through everything downstream of it, so its gradient is a long product of
"how each layer affects the next"; backpropagation assembles those products
efficiently by introducing, for each layer, an **error** vector
$\delta_\ell$, computed from the output backwards:

$$
\delta_0 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{\mu}_0} = f(x) - y,
\qquad
\delta_{\ell+1} = g'(\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}) \odot \big(W_\ell^\top \delta_\ell\big),
\tag{BP}
$$

where $g'$ is the derivative of the nonlinearity, $\odot$ multiplies entry by
entry, and $W_\ell^\top$ is the transpose of the weight matrix. Once the
errors are known, each weight's gradient is an **outer product** — the error
at the layer below times the activity at the layer above:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_\ell} = \delta_\ell \, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top .
\tag{BP-W}
$$

:::{note}
Two features of (BP) to remember:

1. The slopes $g'$ are evaluated at the **forward-pass** values
   $\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}$ — the values the network computed on the way in, held
   fixed throughout the backward sweep.
2. (BP) is a **strict ordered chain**: $\delta_{\ell+1}$ cannot be computed
   until $\delta_\ell$ exists. The error is threaded backward through the
   whole network in sequence — a *global* procedure, so no weight's update
   can be computed from purely local quantities.

Predictive coding will have to reproduce (BP) and (BP-W) **without** that
ordered global chain — using only each layer's immediate neighbours. That is
the whole challenge.
:::

## 2. Classical predictive coding

**Predictive coding** (PC) is a different way to organise the same
computation, drawn from theoretical neuroscience. Instead of activations, each
layer holds **beliefs**: a belief is a guess (a **mean**, written $\mu$) plus
a confidence in that guess (a **precision**, written $\pi$ — the inverse of a
variance, so high precision means high confidence). Each layer *predicts* the
layer below it through the very same forward map {eq}`eq-forward`,
$\hat{\mu}_\ell = W_\ell \, g(\mu_{\ell+1})$, with $g$ now read as the
**coupling function** — the nonlinearity a layer applies when it predicts the
layer below. The mismatch between what a layer predicted and what it observed
is the **prediction error**

$$
\delta_\ell = \mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell.
$$

(The letter $\delta$ is reused deliberately: this locally defined prediction
error will turn out to coincide with backpropagation's error under the right
condition — a *result*, not an assumption.) How beliefs are corrected from
prediction errors, weighted by precision, is the subject of the
[theory notebook](0.1-Theory.ipynb).

The whole network is scored by one quantity, the **variational free energy**
$F$ — for Gaussian beliefs, simply the sum of every layer's squared prediction
error weighted by its precision:

$$
F = \sum_\ell \frac{\pi_\ell}{2}\,\big(\mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell\big)^2 .
$$

A confident layer (high $\pi_\ell$) is penalised more for the same error, so
it "insists" on its predictions; an unsure layer tolerates mismatch. Learning
happens in two nested motions, both descending this same $F$:

1. **Beliefs settle.** *Clamp* the observation on the output layer (fix
   $\mu_0 = y$; a clamped observation is just a belief that is not allowed to
   move), hold the weights fixed, and let every interior belief slide down the
   free energy, $\dot{\mu}_\ell = -\partial F / \partial \mu_\ell$. Each
   belief is pulled toward its own top-down prediction and pushed by the
   prediction error of the layer below, gated by the local slope of $g$. In
   the textbook variant this relaxation runs for many iterations, until the
   beliefs reach an equilibrium.
2. **Weights nudge.** Each connection then adjusts from purely *local*
   quantities — the precision-weighted error on one side,
   $e_\ell := \hat{\pi}_\ell\,(\mu_\ell - \hat{\mu}_\ell) = \hat{\pi}_\ell\,\delta_\ell$,
   and the activity on the other:

$$
\Delta W_\ell \;\propto\; e_\ell \; g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top .
$$

(The hat on $\hat{\pi}_\ell$ marks the *predicted* precision — the layer's
confidence in its own prediction, before seeing evidence. In the textbook
scheme precisions are fixed parameters, so the predicted and the updated
precision are one and the same number and the hat is immaterial; in the
Hierarchical Gaussian Filter, next, the two come apart — and the whole of §3
turns on the difference.)

This weight rule is **Hebbian** — "cells that fire together wire together":
the update to a connection is a product of the two quantities available at its
two ends. Nothing in it sweeps backward through the network; no part ever
sees the whole model. That locality is the point of predictive coding.

### 2.1 The Hierarchical Gaussian Filter's exact rules: one sweep, not a settling loop

PyHGF's networks do not run the textbook settling loop. The Hierarchical
Gaussian Filter replaces it with a *single closed-form sweep* — predict once,
correct each belief once, nudge the weights once — using the exact update
equations of the generalised HGF {cite:p}`weber:2023`. In its language, **the
layer above is the "value parent" of the layer below**: the parent's belief
generates the child's prediction through {eq}`eq-forward`. When a child layer $\ell$
produces a prediction error, its parent $\ell+1$ updates its belief using
only three things it already has — its own prediction, the connection
$W_\ell$, and the child's error.

First, the child packages its prediction error together with its own
confidence into the **precision-weighted prediction error** it sends upward:

$$
e_\ell := \hat{\pi}_\ell \, \delta_\ell .
$$ (eq-pwpe)

The parent then updates its **precision**, adding a "bottom-up" contribution
routed through the connection:

$$
\pi_{\ell+1} \;=\; \underbrace{\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}}_{\text{parent's prior confidence}}
\;+\; \underbrace{\pi^{\mathrm{BU}}_{\ell+1}}_{\text{arriving from the child}},
\qquad \pi^{\mathrm{BU}}_{\ell+1} \propto \hat{\pi}_\ell .
$$ (eq-precision-update)

The one fact about $\pi^{\mathrm{BU}}$ needed later is written on the right:
**the bottom-up precision is proportional to the child's precision
$\hat{\pi}_\ell$.**

:::{dropdown} The exact form of $\pi^{\mathrm{BU}}$
Concretely, $\pi^{\mathrm{BU}}_{\ell+1} = J^\top \hat{\pi}_\ell J$ with
$J = W_\ell\,\mathrm{diag}(g')$ the local sensitivity of the child's
prediction to the parent — but only the proportionality to $\hat{\pi}_\ell$
matters for what follows.
:::

Finally, the parent updates its **mean** by taking a step, sized by one over
its *posterior* precision, in the direction of the routed child error:

$$
\mu_{\ell+1} \;=\; \hat{\mu}_{\ell+1} \;+\; \frac{1}{\pi_{\ell+1}}\;
g'(\mu_{\ell+1}) \odot \big(W_\ell^\top e_\ell\big).
$$ (eq-mean-update)

:::{admonition} The one subtlety, planted here for later
:class: important

The step in {eq}`eq-mean-update` is divided by the **posterior** precision
$\pi_{\ell+1}$ from {eq}`eq-precision-update` — not by the prior confidence
$\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}$. The whole of §3 turns on that difference.
:::

Notice what these rules are and are not. Each is built only from a layer and
its immediate neighbour: no quantity is threaded across the whole network,
and there is no forced output-to-input ordering. That locality is the prize
predictive coding offers — and it raises the obvious question: can such local
rules possibly match the gradients that backpropagation's global sweep
computes?

## 3. When predictive coding equals backpropagation

There is a specific, well-understood point in the landscape of predictive-coding 
models where PC weight updates recover BP. The existence of this point has actually 
been a central argument in favor of PC, but it is crucial to understand that PC can 
have a range of behaviours around that point that makes it genuily more interesting. 
In this section, we state the condition, proves the equivalence, and verifies it 
numerically.

(silent-interior)=
### 3.1 The condition: a silent interior

Predictive coding reproduces exactly backpropagation's gradients when two things 
hold: the interior is **silent**, and every interior layer relays the routed error 
at full strength rather than attenuating it. We call silent interior the behaviour 
of hidden activities when they stay at their forward-pass values even after the 
observation arrives at the output: $\mu_\ell \approx \hat{\mu}_\ell$, up to a 
shift that vanishes as the interior precision grows.

When the interior is silent (i.e., when the activation of the forward pass match 
the activation of the backward pass in PyHGF), the only effective prediction error 
in the network is at the output. Every interior layer merely relays the forward pass, 
and each connection's local update (error on one side) ×
(activity on the other) is precisely the chain-rule gradient.  If instead the hidden 
layers moved to satisfy the target, their errors would no longer be backpropagation's 
errors, and the two would diverge.

In the classical settling formulation, where precisions are fixed parameters, the 
silent interior is the whole condition {cite:p}`whittington:2017,song:2020,millidge:2020`. 
In the HGF, precisions themselves update by {eq}`eq-precision-update` and reflect 
activation uncertainty. Therefor, error is more likely to propagate following a BP 
gradient in a network made of confident activation, but as errors appear, and as we 
are logging this uncertainty, the gradient of learning will diverge from BP.

It is therefor straightforward to recover BP gradient by pinning neurons precision 
to some high number, and disregard any precision update. This is a way to learn efficiently 
using PyHGF only backend, but is also a more limited way.

:::{note}
"Silent" does not mean *frozen*: the interior still shifts by an
infinitesimal amount, just enough to carry the gradient onward. The movement is just too 
small to change *what the activations represent*, bt is never literally zero.
:::

```{prf:theorem} Pinned single-sweep predictive coding reproduces backpropagation
:label: thm-pc-backprop

Run the single belief propagation with every interior precision **pinned** to a large 
constant. Seed the output with
the loss gradient $e_0 = f(x) - y$ for a squared-error output; $\text{one-hot} - \text{softmax}$ 
for a categorical-head. Then the upward messages obey

$$
e_{\ell+1} \;=\; g'(\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}) \odot \big(W_\ell^\top\, e_\ell\big),
\tag{★}
$$

which is exactly backpropagation's error recursion (BP). By induction down the stack, 
$e_\ell$ equals the backpropagation error $\delta_\ell$ at every layer, so the local 
Hebbian weight step reproduces the backpropagation gradient node for node.

$$
\Delta W_\ell \propto e_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top
= \delta_\ell\, g(\mu_{\ell+1})^\top
= \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_\ell}
$$

```

(precision-ratio)=
### 3.2 The proof: the precision ratio $r$

**Goal.** Rewrite the HGF mean update {eq}`eq-mean-update` as a recursion in the upward 
messages $e_\ell$, compare it line for line with backpropagation's (BP), and close the 
gaps between the two. The whole argument turns on one quantity:

```{prf:definition} Precision ratio
:label: def-precision-ratio

The ratio of a layer's prior (predicted) to posterior precision,

$$
r_\ell = \frac{\hat{\pi}_\ell}{\pi_\ell} \in (0,1].
$$

It is at most $1$ because the posterior precision is always at least the prior (observing a 
new event cannot make you more uncertain than you were before observing it). Pinning the 
precision is a way to sets $r_\ell = 1$ exactly.
```

```{prf:proof}
**Step 1 — read off the parent's own error.** The parent's prediction error
is just its displacement from its prediction. Subtract $\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}$
from both sides of {eq}`eq-mean-update`:

$$
\delta_{\ell+1} = \mu_{\ell+1} - \hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}
= \frac{1}{\pi_{\ell+1}}\; g'(\mu_{\ell+1}) \odot \big(W_\ell^\top e_\ell\big).
$$ (eq-displacement)

**Step 2 — form the parent's own upward message.** By the definition of the
upward message {eq}`eq-pwpe`, the parent's message to *its* parent is
$e_{\ell+1} = \hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}\,\delta_{\ell+1}$. Multiply
{eq}`eq-displacement` by $\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}$:

$$
\boxed{\;e_{\ell+1} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}}{\pi_{\ell+1}}}_{\displaystyle r_{\ell+1}}\;
g'(\mu_{\ell+1}) \odot \big(W_\ell^\top e_\ell\big)\;}
\tag{HGF}
$$

The gain in front is exactly the precision ratio of
{prf:ref}`def-precision-ratio`.

**Step 3 — compare with backpropagation.** Put (HGF) next to (BP):

$$
\text{HGF:}\quad e_{\ell+1} = r_{\ell+1}\, g'(\mu_{\ell+1}) \odot (W_\ell^\top e_\ell),
\qquad
\text{BP:}\quad \delta_{\ell+1} = g'(\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}) \odot (W_\ell^\top \delta_\ell).
$$

They are the **same recursion** except for exactly two discrepancies:

- **(D1)** a scalar gain $r_{\ell+1}\le 1$ that the HGF applies at each step
  and backpropagation does not;
- **(D2)** the HGF evaluates the slope $g'$ at the *current belief*
  $\mu_{\ell+1}$, whereas backpropagation evaluates it at the *forward-pass*
  value $\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}$.

If **(D1)** can be driven to $r_{\ell+1}=1$ and **(D2)** to
$\mu_{\ell+1}=\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}$, the two recursions become identical, they
share the same seed ($e_0=\delta_0$), and therefore $e_\ell = \delta_\ell$ at
every layer — the theorem. Closing the gaps is where the naïve argument goes
wrong, so it is done slowly.

**Gap (D2) closes immediately: the silent interior.** Look again at the
displacement {eq}`eq-displacement`: the parent moves by an amount divided by its (large)
posterior precision. Make the interior precisions huge,
$\hat{\pi}_\ell = \Pi \to \infty$. Then

$$
\delta_{\ell+1} = \frac{1}{\pi_{\ell+1}}\,g'\odot(W_\ell^\top e_\ell)
= O(1/\Pi) \longrightarrow 0.
$$

A very confident layer barely moves off its prediction. So
$\mu_{\ell+1}\to\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1}$, the beliefs stay at their forward-pass
values, and $g'(\mu_{\ell+1})\to g'(\hat{\mu}_{\ell+1})$ — the silent
interior ({prf:ref}`def-silent-interior`), now derived rather than assumed.

**Gap (D1) does *not* close just from large $\Pi$ — the trap.** The tempting
next sentence is: "and with $\Pi$ huge,
$r_{\ell+1}=\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}/\pi_{\ell+1}\to1$ too." This is false — and it
is the most important point on this page: "high precision recovers
backpropagation" is *true*, but this naïve reason for it is *wrong*.
Substitute the precision update {eq}`eq-precision-update`, remembering that
the bottom-up term is
proportional to the *child's* precision:

$$
r_{\ell+1} = \frac{\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1}}{\hat{\pi}_{\ell+1} + \pi^{\mathrm{BU}}_{\ell+1}}
= \frac{\Pi}{\Pi + c\,\hat{\pi}_\ell}
= \frac{\Pi}{\Pi + c\,\Pi} = \frac{1}{1+c},
$$

where $c$ is a coupling constant ($\propto \lVert g'W_\ell\rVert^2$). If
**every** interior layer has the same large precision, the two $\Pi$'s
**cancel**: the bottom-up precision that separates the prior from the
posterior scales with $\Pi$ exactly as fast as the prior does, and
$r_{\ell+1}$ is stuck at a coupling-dependent constant $1/(1+c)$ that does
not depend on $\Pi$ at all. Making the network more confident does not push
$r$ toward $1$. (§3.3 checks this directly across eight orders of magnitude.)

**What actually closes (D1): pinning.** The correct condition is stronger and
more specific than "large $\Pi$": hold each interior layer's posterior
precision fixed at its prior value ({prf:ref}`def-pinning`). With the
posterior precision pinned to the prior,
$r_\ell = \hat{\pi}_\ell/\pi_\ell = 1$ **by construction, exactly, at every
interior layer** — no limit required. And pinning to a *large* value
simultaneously keeps the interior silent, because the mean step in
{eq}`eq-mean-update` is still divided by the large $\Pi$. One act does both
jobs: it sets $r=1$ and freezes the beliefs. With both gaps closed, (HGF)
collapses to (★), which is backpropagation's error recursion (BP) exactly.
```

:::{note}
The same subtlety in one line: the prior precision $\hat{\pi}$ is the layer's
confidence *conditional on* its parent; the posterior precision $\pi$ is its
*marginal* confidence after hearing the child. Pinning declares these equal —
the conditional-vs-marginal distinction that recurs throughout the HGF,
appearing here as the coordinate $r=\hat{\pi}/\pi$.
:::

**The cancellation, in words.** Each interior belief moved by only
$\delta_\ell = e_\ell/\Pi$, a factor $1/\Pi$ smaller than a full step. But the
message it sends is $e_\ell = \hat{\pi}_\ell\delta_\ell = \Pi\cdot(e_\ell/\Pi)$
— the $\Pi$ divides on the way in and multiplies on the way out, and the
product is exactly the backpropagation error, independent of $\Pi$. A layer
that barely twitched still transmits a full-strength gradient, because *how
far it moved* and *how loudly that movement is reported* are reciprocal in
the precision.

:::{admonition} Why this is not a reimplementation of backpropagation
:class: important dropdown

Dropped to leading order in $1/\Pi$, the pinned update is one reverse sweep —
the same reverse-mode vector–Jacobian product `jax.grad` would build. (In
code, the mean update's division by the pinned precision and the
`precision_weighted` weight gradient's multiplication by it —
`pyhgf.updates.vectorized.learning` — are the two halves of the cancellation
above.) But PyHGF never differentiates a graph; it reaches those numbers by
local message passing on a generative model. The equivalence is a
theorem-level coincidence of two derivations — variational inference on one
side, the chain rule on the other — and it holds *only* with the interior
pinned and silent: release the precisions and $r$ drops below $1$, (★)
breaks, and the update leaves backpropagation behind (§4).
:::

### 3.3 Numerical verification

Two checks exist, and they measure different things.

**The mathematical statement.** A 5-layer $\tanh$ network (three interior
hidden layers), random weights, squared-error output; the one-sweep update of
§2.1 compared against backpropagation, with backpropagation itself first
confirmed against finite differences to $7\times10^{-10}$:

```{code-block} text
:caption: One-sweep HGF update vs backpropagation — 5-layer tanh network, squared-error output
:emphasize-lines: 9-12

A) PINNED one-sweep vs backprop:  relative error = 0.00e+00
   interior r = [1.0, 1.0, 1.0, 1.0]           (exactly 1)
   max interior belief displacement |δ| = 1.99e-04   (silent, ~1/Π with Π=1e4)

B) UNPINNED one-sweep vs backprop: relative error = 9.05e-01   (diverges)
   interior r = [0.9999, 0.3352, 0.4612, 0.8146]     (< 1)

C) uniform precision, UNPINNED, does r→1 as Π grows?
     Π=1e+02: interior r = [0.9928, 0.3352, 0.4612, 0.8146]
     Π=1e+04: interior r = [0.9999, 0.3352, 0.4612, 0.8146]
     Π=1e+08: interior r = [1.0,    0.3352, 0.4612, 0.8146]
   → deep-interior r frozen at coupling constants regardless of Π:  NO.
```

Result **A** confirms the theorem: pinning gives $r=1$ exactly, a silent
interior, and machine-zero agreement with backpropagation. **B** confirms
that letting the precision update {eq}`eq-precision-update` run breaks the
correspondence. **C** is
the decisive check on the trap: eight orders of magnitude of uniform
precision never move the deep-interior $r$ — only the first interior layer
approaches $1$, and only because its child is the low-precision output. The
condition is *pinning*, not a large-precision limit.

**The library.** In the pinned configuration, one full PyHGF step reproduces
backpropagation to ~1e-5 relative error — the parity numbers of the
*Verifying a port* section of the
[implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md), run through the
actual vectorized kernels rather than the idealized recursion above. Those
numbers are why the worked Transformer of the implementation page follows its
backpropagation twin's loss curve so closely.

:::{admonition} What is established, and what is specific to PyHGF
:class: seealso

The equivalence itself is established prior work: that silent-interior
predictive coding reproduces backpropagation is the theorem of
{cite:t}`whittington:2017`, made exact by {cite:t}`song:2020` and extended to
arbitrary computation graphs by {cite:t}`millidge:2020`. PyHGF neither
discovers nor reimplements that result. What is specific here is the *route*
to it — the equivalence is expressed through the Hierarchical Gaussian
Filter's own confidence (precision) machinery, where a single precision both
silences the interior and rescales the frozen shift back to a full-size
gradient (the cancellation of §3.2), and it is reached in a *single sweep*
rather than the textbook settle-to-equilibrium loop. The contribution is the
confidence framing — the coordinate $r=\hat{\pi}/\pi$ and the pinning
condition — and the map of where the silent regime ends (§4–§5), not the
backpropagation correspondence.
:::

(two-levers)=
### 3.4 Two ways to silence the interior

Two levers produce a silent interior, and they are the same physics seen from
opposite sides.

- **Pin confidence high (the toolbox's lever).** A belief's updated value is a
  confidence-weighted compromise between its top-down prediction (its
  forward-pass value) and the bottom-up error. Pinning the hidden precision to
  an enormous constant sticks the compromise to the prediction: the activities
  cannot move — and because the precision is *held* at its prior value rather
  than merely set large, $r=1$ as well (§3.2). This is an *explicit* dial —
  the $10^4$ in the parity recipe of the
  [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md).
- **Make the network wide and shallow (the scaling lever).** With ordinary
  unit confidences, whether the settled activities land near the forward pass
  depends on the network's shape. A scaling analysis of predictive coding
  {cite:p}`innocenti:2026` shows that the settled
  ("equilibrated") energy PC learns on is a *rescaled* loss
  $L(\theta) / s(\theta)$, with a rescaling
  $s(\theta) = 1 + \Theta(\text{depth} / \text{width})$. When width far
  exceeds depth, $s \to 1$, the rescaled loss becomes the plain loss, and
  PC's gradients converge to backpropagation's. Wide-and-shallow silences the
  interior by geometry rather than by decree.

The confidence dial and the rescaling $s(\theta)$ are the same knob — "how
strongly the interior can be pulled off the forward pass." The toolbox makes
it explicit; the scaling result lets it emerge from architecture. The worked
Transformer of the [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)
is wide (64–128 features) and shallow (two blocks), so it sits in the
$s \to 1$, silent-interior basin on both counts — which is why it matches its
backpropagation twin so cleanly.

(prospective-configuration)=
## 4. The opposite end: prospective configuration

Is a silent interior a limitation? It is the *opposite* of a distinct idea,
**prospective configuration** {cite:p}`Song2024`: letting the hidden
activities relax to a genuinely new, target-consistent configuration *before*
any weight changes. That reconfiguration is argued to reduce interference
between updates and to help online and continual learning — behaviour
backpropagation, which never moves its activations, cannot express.

The two are not in conflict; they are the two ends of one axis — and the
precision ratio $r = \hat{\pi}/\pi$ of §3.2 is that axis's coordinate:

- **$r \equiv 1$ (pinned): silent interior** (activities barely move) → PC =
  backpropagation → *no* prospective configuration.
- **$r \to 0$ (bottom-up precision dominating): moving interior** (activities
  reconfigure) → PC ≠ backpropagation → prospective configuration, with its
  potential benefits.

Releasing the pin restores the bottom-up precision term in
{eq}`eq-precision-update`, so
$r_\ell < 1$ and each layer *attenuates* the routed error rather than
relaying it intact. The interior beliefs move by $O(1)$ instead of $O(1/\Pi)$
— they genuinely reconfigure to fit the target — and the slopes $g'$ are
taken at the moved values. Both discrepancies (D1) and (D2) of §3.2 switch
back on together, and the update slides continuously away from
backpropagation toward prospective configuration.

The difference is the *scale* of movement. The silent regime allows only
*messenger* movement — the infinitesimal shift that transmits the gradient
(§3.1). Prospective configuration needs *reconfiguration* movement —
substantial enough to rearrange the representation toward the target. A
network cannot do both at once: reproducing backpropagation requires
silencing the interior; prospective configuration requires the reverse.

:::{admonition} Clamping the output is already reconfiguration
:class: warning

Hard-clamping the output belief to the target and letting the interior settle
— the textbook scheme of §2 — already belongs to the reconfiguring regime,
not the silent one. The backpropagation correspondence of §3 is a property of
the HGF's single closed-form sweep with pinned interior precisions, not of
every relaxation scheme one might run on the same model.
:::

## 5. When predictive coding actually beats backpropagation

There is one regime where PC provably learns in *fewer* steps than
backpropagation: **faster saddle-to-saddle escape in deeper-than-wide
networks.** Deep networks trained from a small initialisation descend the
loss in a staircase — long flat plateaus (**saddle points**, where the
gradient nearly vanishes and training crawls) punctuated by sudden drops.
Because PC learns on the rescaled loss $L / s(\theta)$, its gradient carries
an extra term that is nonzero *even where the plain loss is flat*, so it
slides off those plateaus sooner: the degenerate, slow-to-escape saddles of
the loss become benign in the rescaled energy {cite:p}`innocenti:2024`.

This is the mirror image of §3.4 — the advantage needs $s(\theta)$ far from
1, i.e. **depth greater than width**, the very regime the silent-interior
condition excludes. It comes with hard limits: it relies on the standard
parameterisation, which is numerically unstable at scale, and it
**disappears for residual networks**, so it does not apply to transformers
however deep — the residual shortcuts remove the saddles it exploits.

## 6. Where PyHGF sits

PyHGF is *single-sweep* predictive coding — predict once, correct beliefs
once, nudge weights once per batch — with the confidence machinery kept
explicit throughout. On the map above:

- **Parity is an anchor, not a ceiling to beat.** The parity recipe
  deliberately parks the learners at the silent-interior end ($r \equiv 1$),
  where PC = backpropagation. That is what makes it a rigorous correctness
  check (the *Verifying a port* section of the
  [implementation page](0.6-Deep_networks_implementation.md)) — and also why
  nothing here should be expected to *out-learn* backpropagation. Matching it
  is the goal, and matching it is what the numbers show (the worked
  Transformer).
- **The worked Transformer is in the flat basin by construction.** Being
  wide, shallow, and residual puts it squarely where PC = backpropagation.
  Widening it keeps it there; deepening it does *not* open the saddle-escape
  advantage, because residual models lack that regime (§5). Distinctive PC
  behaviour will not appear by tuning this model.
- **LayerNorm is frozen for a concrete reason.** Making LayerNorm a genuine
  settling belief layer breaks the correspondence even at large width — the
  normalisation's coupling across a token disturbs the settling the
  silent-interior condition depends on {cite:p}`innocenti:2026`. Keeping it a
  frozen relay (see the worked Transformer) sidesteps this; the paths to let
  its affine learn without reintroducing the problem are open questions.
- **Exploring PC's own behaviour means leaving the silent regime on
  purpose.** Prospective configuration, faster adaptation, and interference
  resistance all live at the *moving-interior* end. Reaching it needs three
  things the current scheme lacks: released precisions (letting $r$ drop
  below $1$, so the interior can move), a settling loop (so it relaxes to an
  equilibrium instead of taking one sweep), and non-stationary or continual
  tasks (where the benefit would show). These are open directions — and the
  reason the confidence machinery, a parity device today, is kept.
